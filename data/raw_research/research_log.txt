# Research Log: Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

## 1. What is a Convolutional Neural Network?
* A convolutional neural network (CNN) is a type of feedforward neural network that learns features via filter (or kernel) optimization.
* It is the de-facto standard in deep learning-based approaches to computer vision and image processing.
* CNNs are also known as shift-invariant or space-invariant artificial neural networks. This is based on the shared-weight architecture of the convolution kernels or filters that slide along input features and provide translation-equivariant responses known as feature maps.
* Counter-intuitively, most CNNs are not perfectly invariant to translation due to the downsampling operations they apply.
* They prevent vanishing and exploding gradients (common in earlier neural networks) through regularization that comes from using shared weights over fewer connections.

## 2. History and Evolution
* **Inspiration (1959):** Inspired by the biological model proposed by Hubel & Wiesel, who discovered "simple cells" and "complex cells" in the visual primary cortex and proposed a cascading model for pattern recognition.
* **Neocognitron (1979):** Proposed by Kunihiko Fukushima. It was a hierarchical, multilayered artificial neural network used for Japanese handwritten character recognition. It learned convolutional kernels via unsupervised learning and served as the direct inspiration for modern CNNs.
* **Yann LeCun & LeNet:** Yann LeCun pioneered the use of CNNs for optical character recognition and computer vision, developing architectures like LeNet.
* **AlexNet (2012):** Developed by Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton. It achieved prominence by winning the ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) with a top-5 error rate of 15.3%. It proved that network depth was essential for high performance and demonstrated the feasibility of using GPUs for training large models (60 million parameters, 650,000 neurons).

## 3. How it Works (Architecture & Building Blocks)
* **Convolutional Layers:** Instead of connecting every neuron to every pixel (e.g., 10,000 weights for a 100x100 image), CNNs apply cascaded convolution (or cross-correlation) kernels. For example, only 25 weights are required to process 5x5-sized tiles. Higher-layer features are extracted from wider context windows compared to lower-layer features.
* **Pooling Layers:** A network layer that downsamples and aggregates information dispersed among many vectors into fewer vectors. 
  * Removes redundant information.
  * Reduces computation and memory requirements.
  * Makes the model more robust to small variations in the input.
  * Increases the receptive field of neurons in later layers.
  * Max-pooling is a common technique.
* **Fully Connected Layers:** Typically used at the end of the network for classification.
* **Activation Functions:** Modern CNNs (like AlexNet) popularized the use of non-saturating ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) activation functions, which train faster and better than traditional tanh and sigmoid functions.
* **Typical Structure:** (CONV -> Normalization -> Pooling) repeated -> Fully Connected -> Softmax.

## 4. Applications
CNNs have been applied to process and make predictions from many different types of data (text, images, audio). Specific applications include:
* Image and video recognition
* Image classification and segmentation
* Medical image analysis
* Recommender systems
* Natural language processing (NLP)
* Brain–computer interfaces
* Financial time series forecasting
